Grape Diseases Encyclopedia

Learn about common grape leaf diseases, their symptoms, and effective treatment strategies

Grape diseases can significantly impact vineyard productivity and grape quality. Early detection and proper management are crucial for maintaining healthy vines and ensuring a successful harvest. Below are the common diseases that our system can detect, along with their symptoms, causes, and recommended treatments.

Black Rot

Symptoms

Black rot first appears as small, brownish spots on leaves. As the disease progresses, these spots enlarge and develop a dark border with a tan or brown center. Severely infected leaves may turn yellow and fall off. On fruits, small, round, brown spots appear and quickly expand, causing the grape to shrivel and turn into a hard, black mummy.

Causes

Black rot is caused by the fungus Guignardia bidwellii. The fungus overwinters in mummified berries and infected canes. In spring, spores are released during rainy periods and spread by wind and rain splash.

Treatment

  • Remove and destroy all mummified berries and infected plant material
  • Prune vines to improve air circulation
  • Apply fungicides preventatively, starting at bud break
  • Use fungicides containing captan, myclobutanil, or mancozeb
  • Maintain a regular spray schedule, especially during wet periods

Identification Keys

Leaf Symptoms

Circular lesions with dark borders and tan centers

Fruit Symptoms

Brown circular spots that eventually cause shriveling

Development Stage

Early to mid-growing season

Favorable Conditions

Warm, humid weather with frequent rain

Esca (Black Measles)

Symptoms

Esca is characterized by tiger-stripe patterns on leaves, with yellow or reddish-brown areas between the veins and green bands along the veins. Berries may develop small, dark spots known as "measles." In severe cases, entire vines can suddenly wilt and die, a condition known as "apoplexy."

Causes

Esca is a complex disease caused by multiple fungi, including Phaeomoniella chlamydospora, Phaeoacremonium species, and Fomitiporia mediterranea. These fungi infect the woody tissue of the vine, causing decay and blocking water and nutrient transport.

Treatment

  • There is no cure for infected vines
  • Remove and destroy severely infected vines
  • Use clean pruning tools, disinfecting between vines
  • Protect pruning wounds with fungicides or wound sealants
  • Maintain vine vigor through proper nutrition and irrigation
  • Some success has been reported with trunk renewal techniques

Identification Keys

Leaf Symptoms

Tiger-stripe patterns with yellow/red areas between green veins

Fruit Symptoms

Dark spots on berries resembling measles

Development Stage

Mid to late growing season, often during hot periods

Vine Age

More common in mature vines (8+ years old)

Leaf Blight (Isariopsis Leaf Spot)

Symptoms

Leaf blight appears as small, irregular, reddish-brown spots with yellow halos on the upper leaf surface. As the disease progresses, these spots may enlarge and merge, causing the leaf to dry out and fall off. Severe infections can cause significant defoliation.

Causes

Leaf blight is caused by the fungus Pseudocercospora vitis (formerly known as Isariopsis leaf spot). The fungus overwinters in fallen leaves and can spread rapidly during warm, humid conditions.

Treatment

  • Remove and destroy fallen leaves
  • Improve air circulation through proper pruning and canopy management
  • Apply fungicides containing copper, mancozeb, or azoxystrobin
  • Begin fungicide applications when symptoms first appear
  • Maintain a regular spray schedule during humid conditions

Identification Keys

Leaf Symptoms

Irregular reddish-brown spots with yellow halos

Pattern

Spots can merge, causing irregular patches

Development Stage

Mid to late growing season

Favorable Conditions

Warm, humid weather with extended leaf wetness

Healthy Leaves

Characteristics

Healthy grape leaves are typically green, intact, and free from spots, discoloration, or abnormal growth patterns. The leaf surface should be smooth and uniform in color, with well-defined veins. Leaf edges may be serrated but should not show signs of wilting, curling, or necrosis.

Maintaining Healthy Vines

  • Implement proper pruning techniques to improve air circulation
  • Maintain balanced nutrition through soil testing and appropriate fertilization
  • Provide adequate but not excessive irrigation
  • Practice preventative fungicide applications during high-risk periods
  • Monitor vines regularly for early signs of disease
  • Control weeds that may harbor pests and diseases
  • Implement integrated pest management strategies

Identification Keys

Leaf Appearance

Uniform green color, smooth surface, well-defined veins

Leaf Texture

Firm, without brittleness or wilting

Growth Pattern

Regular, with consistent leaf size and shape

Fruit Development

Normal cluster formation and berry growth

General Prevention Tips

Implementing these practices can help reduce disease pressure in your vineyard

Plant Selection

Choose disease-resistant grape varieties suitable for your climate and soil conditions.

Vineyard Layout

Plant rows in the direction of prevailing winds to improve air circulation and reduce humidity.

Pruning

Proper pruning improves sunlight penetration and air movement, reducing disease pressure.

Irrigation

Use drip irrigation to keep foliage dry and reduce conditions favorable for disease development.

Sanitation

Remove and destroy infected plant material to reduce disease inoculum in the vineyard.

Monitoring

Regular scouting helps detect diseases early when they're easier to manage.
